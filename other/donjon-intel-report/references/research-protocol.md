# Research Protocol

How to conduct research for a Donjon Intelligence Report. This isn't about finding
information — it's about finding the *right* information and knowing how much to
trust it.

## The Research Mindset

You're building a case file, not writing a Wikipedia article. Every search should
have a purpose. Every source should be evaluated. The goal is to understand the
subject well enough to tell the client something they couldn't easily find themselves.

## Phase 1: Broad Sweep (10-15 searches)

Start wide. Your goal is to map the territory:

1. **The subject itself** — What is it? What does it claim to be?
2. **Who's behind it** — Founders, leaders, board, investors
3. **The money** — Funding, revenue model, financial filings
4. **The criticism** — What do detractors say? What controversies exist?
5. **The ecosystem** — Who are the partners, competitors, adjacent players?

Search queries should be specific and varied:
- `"[subject] funding investors"` — follow the money
- `"[subject] controversy criticism problems"` — find the counter-narrative
- `"[person name] LinkedIn career background"` — personnel research
- `"[company] 990 filing nonprofit"` — financial filings
- `"[subject] site:reddit.com OR site:glassdoor.com"` — unfiltered perspectives
- `"[subject] [year]"` — time-bounded for recent developments

## Phase 2: Source Triangulation (5-10 searches)

Now go deep on the claims that matter most:

1. **Cross-reference key facts** — If source A says the company raised $17M, find
   source B that confirms it. Note when you can't.
2. **Find primary sources** — Press coverage is secondary. The filing, the permit,
   the official announcement is primary. Always try to get one level closer to the
   original.
3. **Check for updates** — Old articles may have been superseded. Search for the
   most recent reporting on key claims.
4. **Verify personnel** — Titles change. People leave. Check that current roles
   are actually current.

## Phase 3: Connection Mapping (3-5 searches)

This is where the real intelligence value lives:

1. **Board overlaps** — Do any board members sit on other relevant boards?
2. **Investment connections** — Did the same VC fund related companies?
3. **Personnel flows** — Did key people come from or go to related organizations?
4. **Political connections** — Any campaign donations, lobbying, or policy involvement?
5. **The supply chain** — Who are their customers? Who are their vendors?

## Source Classification

Every source falls into one of these categories:

| Category | Examples | Trust Level |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Primary** | Government filings, court records, SEC/IRS documents, official permits | Highest — facts of record |
| **Official** | Company press releases, annual reports, official websites | High for facts, lower for claims |
| **Investigative** | Major outlet investigations (NYT, WSJ, ProPublica, local investigative teams) | High — verified by editorial process |
| **News** | Standard news coverage, trade publications | Medium — may be based on press releases |
| **Self-reported** | LinkedIn profiles, personal websites, Crunchbase (user-edited) | Medium-Low — verify key claims |
| **Social/Forum** | Reddit, Glassdoor, Twitter/X | Low for facts, useful for sentiment |
| **Inference** | Your own analytical conclusions | Tag explicitly as inference |

## What "Confidence: HIGH" Actually Means

- You have at least two independent sources confirming the claim
- At least one source is primary or investigative
- The claim is specific and falsifiable (not vague)
- You checked for contradicting information and found none

## What "Confidence: MEDIUM" Actually Means

- You have one credible source, or multiple secondary sources
- The claim is plausible and consistent with other findings
- You couldn't find contradicting information, but you also couldn't fully verify
- Some details may be approximate (e.g., funding amounts from press coverage vs. filings)

## What "Confidence: LOW" Actually Means

- This is analytical inference — a conclusion you're drawing from patterns
- Or it's from a single unverified source
- Or the claim is hard to verify by nature (e.g., "they plan to expand")
- You should explain your reasoning in the Basis column

## Red Flags to Look For

- **PR-only coverage** — If the only articles are rewritten press releases, you don't
  have independent verification of anything
- **Dead links and scrubbed content** — Sometimes more interesting than what's still up
- **Title inflation** — "CEO of a foundation" that has one employee and no revenue
- **Circular sourcing** — Multiple articles that all trace back to the same original source
- **Undisclosed conflicts** — The person writing the glowing review is also an investor
- **Timeline gaps** — Years missing from a LinkedIn profile or company history

## Research Scope by Report Type

| Report Type | Research Depth | Typical Searches |
|-------------|---------------|------------------|
| Organizational Intelligence | Comprehensive | 20-30 |
| Personnel Dossier | Focused | 10-20 |
| Deep Report | Exhaustive | 30-50 |
| Interview Intelligence | Broad + focused | 15-25 |
| Competitive Intelligence | Multi-target | 25-40 |
| Due Diligence | Forensic | 30-50 |

## When to Stop Researching

- You've answered the client's core question
- Additional searches are returning information you already have
- You've checked for the counter-narrative and either found it or confirmed it doesn't exist
- You have enough sources to populate a meaningful confidence table
- You understand the subject well enough to write the Executive Read without looking
  at your notes

If you can't write the Executive Read from memory, you haven't researched enough.
If you're finding the same articles on your fifth search variation, you've researched enough.
